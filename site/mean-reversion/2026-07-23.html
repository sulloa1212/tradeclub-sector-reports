This legend explains the generic methodology — how each structure works and when it fits. Specific trade levels always come from your live brokerage, not this report.

Direction + IV → Structure (Educational Only)
Direction IV Environment Preferred Defined-Risk Structure Why It Fits
Oversold / Upside IV Elevated (fear spike) Cash-secured put Bull put spread (put credit spread) Sell rich put premium; profit if stock stays above your short strike. The credit you collect is your max gain; your spread width minus credit is max loss. Always defined risk.
Oversold / Upside IV Low (calm) Bull call spread (call debit spread) Buy a call closer to the money, sell a higher-strike call to offset cost. Pay a small debit; profit if price rises to your long strike area by expiry.
Overbought / Downside IV Elevated Call credit spread (bear call spread) Sell an at-the-money or near-money call, buy a higher-strike call for protection. Profit if the stock stays below your short strike; risk is capped.
Overbought / Downside IV Low Bear put spread (put debit spread) Buy a put, sell a lower-strike put to reduce cost. Profit if the stock falls; risk is limited to the debit paid.

Technical term definitions (what each measure means):
RSI (14-day)A 0–100 gauge of recent price momentum. Below 30 = oversold (selling may be exhausted); above 70 = overbought (buying may be exhausted). Extremes below 20 / above 80 are more severe.
Bollinger %B (20,2)Measures where price sits within its Bollinger Bands (a 20-day price envelope ±2 standard deviations). Below 0 = outside the lower band (oversold); above 1 = outside the upper band (overbought).
Z-score vs 50-dayHow many standard deviations the current price is from its 50-day average. ±2 is notable; ±3 is extreme. A negative Z-score means below the average; positive means above.
% from 50-day / 200-day SMAHow far (in percent) the current price is above or below its 50- or 200-day simple moving average — the "mean" a reversion trade aims for.
IV (Implied Volatility)The options market's estimate of future price swings. High IV = options are expensive (sell premium). Low IV = options are cheap (buy options). Context: elevated IV after a sharp drop is often the best time for selling put spreads on oversold names.
ATR (Avg True Range)The average daily price range over a set period. A big move expressed as a multiple of ATR (e.g., "5× ATR in 5 days") signals an extreme stretch.

8How to Read / Guardrails

These rules are how this scan avoids presenting a "falling knife" as a clean setup — read them before acting on any name above.

✓ Keep — looks like a reversion candidate:
A sharp move driven by sentiment/positioning excess, not a fundamental impairment. Business model intact. Prior support or moving-average mean is well-defined. IV elevated (favoring premium selling). No imminent binary event (or you've accounted for it).
✗ Reject — falling knife / permanent re-rating risk:
Structural break: fraud, going-concern doubt, dividend cut, failed drug trial, bankruptcy, accounting restatement. Drop of −50%+ with no stabilization. Buyout/merger repricing (stock pinned to deal price, not its mean). Earnings guide that changes the fundamental valuation model permanently (IBM's guide cut is borderline — watch carefully).
⚡ Special handling — vol, leveraged, and futures ETFs:
Long-vol ETPs (VXX, UVXY): Trade ONLY when stretched UP (fade the spike). A low RSI is structural decay, not a buy. Leveraged / futures ETFs (UCO, BOIL, TQQQ, UNG, USO): Volatility decay + contango/backwardation roll erode value over time. Use for short-term tactical reversion only. K-1 tax note: USO, UNG, UGA, BNO, BOIL, DBC, UCO issue Schedule K-1 tax forms — more complex than a standard 1099. Prefer no-K-1 alternatives (PDBC, BCI) for multi-week holds. Equity proxies (GDX, XLE, XOP, OIH, URA): These track companies, not the commodity. They carry equity beta and can diverge significantly from the underlying commodity price.
