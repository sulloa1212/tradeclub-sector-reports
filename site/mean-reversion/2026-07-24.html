7 Options Structures Legend

How direction and IV level map to educational defined-risk structures. This is general method — the actual results for today's setups are in the tables above.

Oversold + High IV → Premium Selling (defined-risk) Bull put spread: Sell a higher-strike put, buy a lower-strike put. Profits if stock stays above the short strike. IV decay works in your favor. Max loss limited to spread width minus credit received.
Oversold + Low/Moderate IV → Premium Buying (defined-risk) Bull call spread: Buy a lower-strike call, sell a higher-strike call. Pays off on an upward move. Costs a net debit. Max loss = debit paid. Better when IV is lower (cheaper to buy).
Overbought + High IV → Premium Selling (defined-risk) Bear call spread: Sell a lower-strike call, buy a higher-strike call above. Profits if stock stays below the short strike. IV decay works in your favor. Max loss limited to spread width minus credit received.
Overbought + Low/Moderate IV → Premium Buying (defined-risk) Bear put spread: Buy a higher-strike put, sell a lower-strike put. Pays off on a downward move. Costs a net debit. Max loss = debit paid.
What RSI measures RSI (0–100 overbought/oversold gauge): Compares average gains to average losses over 14 days. Below 30 = oversold (selling may be exhausted). Above 70 = overbought (buying may be stretched). Extreme: below 20 / above 80.
What %B (Bollinger) measures Bollinger %B: 0 = at the lower band, 1 = at the upper band. Below 0 = outside the lower band (very oversold). Above 1 = outside the upper band (very overbought). Uses a 20-day window, 2 standard deviations.
What Z-score means Z-score of price vs 50-day mean: How many standard deviations the price is from its average. A Z-score of +2 or –2 means the price is extremely stretched. Most prices spend 95% of the time between –2 and +2.
Composite ranking method Names at the top of each table fire 3+ of these signals simultaneously: RSI extreme + %B outside bands + Z-score >2 + extended move vs. ATR. A name with one signal (RSI only) ranks lower. Quality of thesis (reversion vs. ruin) overrides pure numeric rank.
IV (implied volatility) — high vs. low IV is the market's forecast of how much a stock might move. High IV = option premiums are expensive (good time to sell options, collect premium). Low IV = premiums are cheap (better time to buy options). A "big" expected move is not automatically bad — it is just larger or smaller risk to size appropriately.

8 Guardrails — How to Read This Report Safely

The #1 question to ask before every trade: Is this a reversion or a ruin? A low RSI is necessary but not sufficient. Here is the filter, applied visibly to today's scan.

The Reversion-vs-Ruin Filter

Reject any name where:

  • Structural break: Fraud, going-concern doubt, failed drug trial, bankruptcy, restatement. None of the names in today's tables have this. Always check before trading.
  • Enormous drop with no stabilization: A –50%+ move in days with no floor. TSLA is –14% in one day — extreme, but still a large-cap with $37B in cash. Not a ruin. Still a caution case — size small.
  • Buyout/deal repricing: Stock pinned to a deal price, not its mean. None present today.
  • Permanent regime change: TSLA's negative free cash flow and GOOGL's CapEx are concerns — but neither is a solvency issue. They are profitable but investing heavily. The bear case is "this lasts longer than expected," not "company fails." Monitor carefully.

Special ETF Caveats

  • K-1 Tax Forms: USO, DBC, UCO, BOIL, most CurrencyShares ETFs, and some commodity ETPs issue a Schedule K-1 tax form — more complex tax filing. Prefer alternatives: PDBC (no K-1) for broad commodities, XLE/XOP (equity, no K-1) for oil exposure. All K-1 names are flagged in the tables.
  • Leverage and futures decay: TQQQ, SQQQ, UCO, SCO, BOIL, KOLD, TBT, UVXY, UVIX are leveraged or futures-based ETFs. They carry volatility decay (leverage drag) or contango roll costs. Use only for short-term tactical reversion — not multi-week holds. For longer holds, use the unlevered fund or equity-sector proxy.
  • Proxy ≠ spot: GDX/GDXJ track gold miners (companies), not gold spot. XOP/OIH track oil company stocks, not WTI crude. These carry equity beta and can diverge meaningfully from the underlying commodity — especially on a broad market sell-off.
  • Long-vol ETPs (VXX, UVXY, VIXY, UVIX): Only trade when stretched UP on a volatility spike — then fade the spike. Never buy them as "oversold." Structural contango decay grinds them lower over time. A low RSI on VXX means decay, not opportunity.
  • Inverse-vol ETPs (SVXY, SVIX): Mirror of the above — they grind UP and crater on vol spikes. Recovery after a vol crash is their reversion setup, not fading a low reading.
