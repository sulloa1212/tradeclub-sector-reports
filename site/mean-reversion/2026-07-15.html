7Options Structures Legend

This legend explains the generic method — what each structure is and when it fits. The specific candidates and results are in the tables above.

Direction + IV → Structure (educational only — paper-trade first):
Oversold + IV Elevated → Bull Put Spread / Cash-Secured Put

Sell a put below current price (and buy a lower-strike put for protection). You collect premium upfront. Works best when you expect the stock to hold above your short strike. The high IV means more premium collected. Defined maximum loss = spread width minus premium received.

Oversold + IV Low → Bull Call Spread / Long Call

Buy a call (or buy a lower call, sell a higher one). You pay premium hoping the stock rises. Low IV makes buying options cheaper. Defined max loss = premium paid.

Overbought + IV Elevated → Bear Call Spread / Call Credit Spread

Sell a call above current price and buy a higher-strike call for protection. You collect premium if the stock stays below your short strike. The high IV means more premium to collect. Max loss = spread width minus premium received.

Overbought + IV Low → Bear Put Spread / Put Debit Spread

Buy a put below current price and sell a lower-strike put. You pay a net debit hoping the stock falls. Low IV makes puts cheaper. Max loss = debit paid.

Technical indicators used in this report:
RSI (Relative Strength Index, 14-day)

A 0–100 momentum gauge. Above 70 = overbought (buying may be exhausted); below 30 = oversold (selling may be exhausted). Extreme: >80 / <20.

Bollinger %B (20-period, 2 standard deviations)

Measures where price sits within its volatility band. Above 1.0 = price is above the upper band (stretched high); below 0 = price is below the lower band (stretched low).

% vs 50d / 200d SMA

How far the current price is above or below its 50-day or 200-day simple moving average (the "mean" it may revert toward). Large positive = overbought; large negative = oversold.

Z-score (50-day)

How many standard deviations price is from its 50-day mean. Z > +2 or < −2 is statistically unusual and often reverts. Used as a composite confirmation alongside RSI.

IV (Implied Volatility)

The options market's estimate of how much a stock might move. Elevated IV → option premium is expensive → favor selling premium (credit spreads). Low IV → options are cheap → favor buying (debit spreads).

ATR (Average True Range)

The average daily price swing over 14 days. A move of 5× ATR in a few days is unusually large and is one signal of a stretched setup.

8How to Read / Guardrails

Reversion vs. Ruin filter — the most important check:

A low RSI alone is not enough to act. This report applies a filter to reject "falling knives." A name is rejected if: (1) The drop is driven by fraud, going-concern doubt, dividend cut, failed trial, bankruptcy, or accounting restatement. (2) The drop is −50%+ in days with no stabilization. (3) It is a buyout repricing (stock pinned to deal price). (4) A CFO departure accompanies a guidance cut. IBM and Pentair were rejected today on these grounds.

Special handling — Vol, Leveraged & Futures ETPs:
  • Long-vol ETPs (VXX, UVXY): Trade only when they spike UP (fade the spike). A low RSI on these is contango/leverage decay — never "oversold."
  • Leveraged ETFs (UCO, SCO, TQQQ, SQQQ, TBT): Volatility decay means they deviate from any stable mean. Short-term tactical use only; prefer unlevered for multi-week holds.
  • Equity proxies (GDX, XOP, XLE, URA): These track companies, not the commodity — they carry stock market beta and can diverge from the underlying spot price.
  • K-1 tax flag: USO, DBC, UNG, UCO issue a Schedule K-1 form at tax time, adding complexity. Prefer PDBC or XLE alternatives where the exposure allows.
  • Futures-based ETFs (USO, UNG): Roll mechanics (contango/backwardation) affect returns. USO is in mild backwardation now — favorable for long exposure short-term, but still carries roll risk.
