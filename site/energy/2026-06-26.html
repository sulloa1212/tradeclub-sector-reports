🌍 Dominant Geopolitical Driver: The US-Iran Peace Deal & Oil Supply Shock Reversal

What defined Energy in 2026 was the US-Iran war, which effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz from late March onward, cutting ~11 million barrels/day of Middle East production and spiking Brent to $126+ and WTI to $110+ by April–May. XLE surged to a 52-week high of $63.46 as the war premium built.

Now the setup is reversing rapidly: A US-Iran MOU was signed ~June 14–15, and on June 22, OFAC published General License X — a sweeping 60-day authorization for global buyers to purchase Iranian crude with no volume cap. Saudi tankers have resumed Persian Gulf exports, Qatar issued its first post-war crude tender, and overall Iranian exports are surging. Brent fell 3.5%+ on June 22 alone. WTI is now on track for its third straight weekly drop, sitting at ~$69–70.

However, the deal is not finalized: nuclear policy details remain unresolved, a cargo ship was struck off Oman on June 25, and Iranian hardliners could still sabotage the process. The EIA assumes the Strait normalizes in Q3 2026 but full pre-conflict traffic may not return until early 2027. Supply recovery will be slow even if peace holds.

§4 Cross-Currents & Counter-Arguments

🐂 What Would Make the Bearish Call Wrong?

1. Peace deal collapses. If Iranian hardliners sabotage the MOU, the Strait re-closes, and oil spikes $15–20 overnight. XLE bounces 8–12% in a session. Every short energy position hurts badly.

2. Supply is structurally slow to recover. Even with peace, mine clearance, infrastructure repairs, and tanker backlogs could take months — meaning the EIA's assumption of Q3 Hormuz normalization is optimistic, and oil could bounce back to $80–85 quickly on inventory tightness.

3. US tightness. Cushing stockpiles fell below operational requirements to just 19 million barrels — historically a floor for prices. US domestic tightness could push WTI back up even as Middle East supply returns.

🐻 What Would Make the Bearish Call Right (and Deepen)?

1. Peace deal holds, Hormuz fully reopens. ~11 million b/d of suppressed Middle East production coming back would crush oil prices toward $60–65. XLE could revisit its 52-week low ($42.05) over the medium term.

2. Earnings estimate cuts accelerate. Q2 results (July–August) will show realized oil prices sharply below Q1's war-inflated average of ~$120 Brent. Consensus has not yet fully priced this — the downgrades could cascade.

3. Trump DOJ probe of "big oil" adds headline overhang. Even without legal substance, regulatory scrutiny headaches can weigh on institutional positioning in XOM/CVX.
