7Options Structures Legend

This section explains the "how" — the generic method behind the structure suggestions in the tables above. Every specific number in the tables is the result; this is the framework behind it.

RSI (0–100 scale)
A momentum gauge. Below 30 = oversold (selling may be exhausted). Above 70 = overbought (buying may be exhausted). Below 20 / above 80 = extreme. Not a prediction — stocks can stay stretched for weeks.
Bollinger %B (0–1 scale)
Measures where price sits within its volatility bands. Below 0 = price is below the lower band (stretched down). Above 1 = above the upper band (stretched up). Confirms RSI when both agree.
% from 50-day / 200-day SMA
How far price has strayed from its moving average "mean." Large negative = far below average (oversold). Large positive = far above (overbought). This is the "distance to revert" estimate.
Direction: OVERSOLD + High IV
Bullish-leaning reversion
→ Bull put spread: sell a put near support, buy a lower put. Defined risk; collects premium while IV is high.
→ Cash-secured put: sell a put you'd be willing to own the stock at. Only for accounts with cash to cover.
→ Bull call spread: buy a call near current price, sell a higher call. Defined-risk debit; costs less than buying a naked call.
Direction: OVERBOUGHT + High IV
Bearish-leaning reversion
→ Call credit spread: sell a call above resistance, buy a higher call. Defined risk; collects premium.
→ Bear put spread: buy a put near current price, sell a lower put. Defined-risk debit; works if IV is moderate (not extreme — then selling is better).
IV (implied volatility) note
High IV = options are expensive. Selling spreads (credit spreads) is more capital-efficient in high-IV environments. Low IV = options are cheap. Buying spreads (debit spreads) is more favorable. Always favor defined-risk structures — never sell naked options as a beginner.
Composite ranking method
Names ranking highest are those stretched on 3 or more measures (RSI + %B + distance from 50-day + Z-score where available). A name extreme on only one measure gets a lower rank. Quality of business (not a falling knife) is the second filter.
Reversion vs. Ruin filter
We exclude: fraud, going-concern risk, buyout pins, dividend cuts (structural breaks), or drops of 50%+ with no stabilization. We keep: sentiment-driven selloffs in sound businesses near prior support.
Proxy / K-1 / Leverage flags
Proxy = tracks companies not the commodity (adds equity risk). K-1 = issues a tax form (use PDBC/BCI instead for oil exposure). Leveraged = decays over time; use for short-term tactical only.

8How to Read / Guardrails

Reversion-vs-Ruin — the most important rule
A low RSI alone is not a buy signal. Before considering any oversold name, ask: "Is this down because of sentiment or because the business is broken?" A drug trial failure, fraud allegation, going-concern notice, or permanent demand destruction is NOT a reversion setup — it is a falling knife. Only proceed when the move looks like positioning excess, not a fundamental regime change.
Earnings / Event Binary Rule
Any name with an earnings report or major event inside 14 days should be treated with extra caution. IV spikes before events — meaning options are expensive. Do NOT buy calls or puts outright into a binary event. Either wait until after the report (when IV drops) or use a defined-risk spread that caps your max loss. This applies especially to SOXX, SMH, NVDA, NOC, and SPCX right now.

  • Proxy ETFs carry equity beta. URA, GDX, GDXJ, XOP, COPX, MOO track companies — not the commodity itself. They can diverge sharply from spot prices during earnings, corporate events, or broad market selloffs.
  • K-1 tax alert. USO, UNG, UGA, DBC, UCO, and most single-commodity futures ETFs issue a Schedule K-1 at tax time. This complicates your taxes and may arrive late. Prefer PDBC (no K-1) for broad commodity exposure.
  • Leverage decay. 2x and 3x ETFs (SOXL, UCO, BOIL, TBT, etc.) lose value through daily rebalancing and volatility drag over time. Do NOT hold these for weeks expecting to match 2x the underlying's long-run return.
  • Long-vol ETPs (VXX, UVXY) fade only on spikes. They decay continuously in normal markets. A low VXX RSI is structural decay — not a buy signal. Only trade these when they are stretched UP on a fear event.
