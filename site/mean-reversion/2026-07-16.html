7Options Structures — Educational Legend

This legend explains the educational options ideas used throughout the report. Always paper-trade first and verify live option chains, strikes, and expirations with your broker before using real money.

RSI (14-day)
A 0–100 momentum gauge measuring how fast a stock moved recently. Above 70 = overbought (moved up fast, may pause). Below 30 = oversold (moved down fast, may bounce). Extremes below 20 or above 80 are rarer and more significant.
Bollinger %B (20,2)
Shows where price sits relative to its upper and lower bands (set 2 standard deviations from the 20-day average). Above 1.0 = above the upper band (stretched high). Below 0.0 = below the lower band (stretched low).
Z-Score vs 50-day
How many standard deviations the current price is from its 50-day average. A Z-score above +2 or below −2 is statistically extreme — price has moved far from its normal range.
IV (Implied Volatility)
The options market's estimate of how much a stock may move. High IV = expensive options (market expects a big move). Low IV = cheap options (market calm). High IV + oversold → consider selling premium (collect the inflated option price). Low IV + oversold → consider buying options (cheaper to own a call/spread).
Cash-Secured Put (CSP)
Oversold + high IV setup. Sell a put option below current price; keep cash in your account to buy shares if assigned. You collect the premium (the option price) upfront. You own the stock at the lower price if it falls there. Max gain: the premium collected. Max loss: the strike price minus premium.
Put Credit Spread
Oversold + high IV, defined-risk version. Sell a put and simultaneously buy a cheaper put at a lower strike. Limits max loss to the difference in strikes minus the premium received. Safer than a naked CSP for beginners.
Bull Call Spread
Oversold + low/moderate IV setup. Buy a call at a lower strike and sell a call at a higher strike. Maximum loss is the premium paid; maximum gain is capped at the spread width minus premium. A defined-risk way to bet on a bounce.
Call Credit Spread
Overbought + high/moderate IV setup. Sell a call above current price and buy a higher-strike call for protection. Collect a net premium. Profit if the stock stays below your short strike. Defined-risk: max loss is the spread width minus premium.
Bear Put Spread
Overbought + low/moderate IV setup. Buy a put at a higher strike and sell a cheaper put at a lower strike. Pay a net debit; profit if the stock falls. Defined-risk directional bet.
Composite Stretch Score (how names are ranked)
Each name is ranked by how many measures confirm the extreme: RSI + %B outside bands + % from 50/200-day SMA + consecutive days of move + size vs ATR. Three or more measures confirming = higher rank. RSI alone is insufficient.

8How to Read This / Guardrails

This section explains the rules used to filter these setups and the special warnings that apply to certain types of instruments.

Reversion vs. Ruin — the most important filter: An oversold RSI is necessary but never sufficient. We actively reject names where the move reflects a structural break (fraud, going-concern, bankruptcy, massive guidance reset with no floor, failed drug trial, accounting restatement). IBM today is a textbook reject: the oversold reading reflects a genuine fundamental re-rating (enterprise IT spending shift to AI hardware), not sentiment excess. Falling knives that stay low RSI for months will destroy a "reversion" portfolio. Always ask: "Is this business still intact?"
Special rules for Vol / Leveraged / Futures ETFs:
  • Long-vol ETPs (VXX, UVXY, VIXY): Only trade when stretched upward (spike fade). Never buy them as an "oversold" reversion — roll decay and contango grind them lower forever.
  • Inverse-vol (SVXY, SVIX): Recovery after a vol spike is their reversion trade; don't fade their uptrends as mean-reversion.
  • Leveraged ETFs (UCO, SCO, TQQQ, SQQQ, BOIL, KOLD, TBT): Volatility decay + roll cost means they deviate from any long-run mean. Use for short-term tactical plays only; for multi-week holds, prefer the unlevered fund or equity-sector proxy.
  • K-1 tax flag: USO, UNG, UGA, UCO, BOIL, DBC, and certain CurrencyShares ETFs issue a Schedule K-1 at tax time, which is more complex than a 1099. Prefer "No K-1" alternatives where available (PDBC, BCI, XLE/XOP for energy exposure).
  • Proxy vs. spot: GDX/GDXJ track gold miners, not gold itself. XOP tracks oil producers. URA tracks uranium miners. These carry equity beta and can diverge substantially from the underlying commodity.
