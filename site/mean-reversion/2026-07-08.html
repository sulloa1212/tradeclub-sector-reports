7Options Structures Legend

This section explains the educational structures mentioned in the tables — what they are and when each fits. These are general definitions; apply them to the specific setup, not as automatic recommendations.

Oversold + High IV → Premium Selling
Put Credit Spread: Sell a put at a lower strike, buy a further-down put for protection. You collect premium if the stock stays above your short strike. Defined max loss = spread width minus premium received. Best when you expect the stock to stop falling or bounce.
Oversold + High IV → Premium Selling
Cash-Secured Put: Sell a put and set aside cash to potentially buy shares. You keep premium if stock stays above strike; if assigned, you own shares at a discount. Works when you want to own the stock at a lower price anyway.
Oversold + Lower IV → Premium Buying
Bull Call Spread: Buy a call at a lower strike, sell a higher-strike call to reduce cost. Profits if the stock rises toward the upper strike. Defined max loss = net premium paid. Use when IV is low and you expect an upside bounce.
Overbought + High IV → Premium Selling
Call Credit Spread: Sell a call at a lower (closer) strike, buy a higher-strike call for protection. You collect premium if the stock stays below your short strike. Defined max loss = spread width minus premium. Use when you expect the stock to stop rising or pull back.
Overbought + Any IV → Directional Put
Bear Put Spread: Buy a put at a higher strike, sell a lower-strike put to reduce cost. Profits if the stock falls toward the lower strike. Defined max loss = net premium paid. Use when you expect a pullback but don't want unlimited risk.

8How to Read This Report & Guardrails

The Reversion-vs-Ruin Filter — the most important concept in this report

A low RSI is necessary but not sufficient for a reversion trade. Stocks can fall from $100 to $50 with an RSI below 30 the whole way. The question is: why did it move, and does that reason imply a return toward the mean?

Keep (reversion candidate): Sentiment/positioning excess over a fundamentally sound business. Move driven by crowded long unwind, sector contagion, or geopolitical spike premium. A clear, well-defined pre-shock mean exists. Business model, earnings, and guidance are unchanged.

Reject (falling knife / permanent re-rating): Fraud, going-concern doubt, dividend cut, failed trial, bankruptcy, debt blowup, accounting restatement. Drop of >50% with no stabilization. Buyout/merger repricing (stock is pinned to deal price, not a mean). Fundamental timeline or business model was actually revised — that is a re-rating, not a reversion.

Proxy vs Spot — always know what you own
  • GDX, GDXJ, XOP, COPX, URA, MOO, SIL: These track companies, not the commodity itself. They carry equity beta and can diverge from underlying spot prices — sometimes dramatically.
  • USO, BNO, UNG, UGA, UCO: These track futures contracts, not physical commodities. Monthly roll costs (contango/backwardation) mean the ETF price can diverge from spot even when spot barely moves.
  • K-1 tax forms: USO, UNG, UGA, BNO, DBC, UCO, BOIL, COPER all issue Schedule K-1 at tax time (more complex than a 1099). Prefer No-K-1 alternatives (XLE, XOP, PDBC, BITO) where the exposure allows.
Leveraged ETF Warning

Leveraged ETFs (UCO, SCO, BOIL, KOLD, TQQQ, SQQQ, TBT, UVXY, UVIX) are built for single-day tactical use. Daily leverage reset + volatility decay + roll costs make them wealth destroyers when held for weeks. If your thesis is multi-week, use the unleveraged version or the underlying equity sector.
