7Options Structures Legend

This section explains the generic method — the how behind the structure recommendations above. Specific results and reads for each name are in the tables in Sections 2–5.

RSI (Relative Strength Index)

A 0–100 momentum gauge. Below 30 = selling pressure extreme (oversold). Above 70 = buying pressure extreme (overbought). Below 20 / above 80 = extreme. RSI alone is not enough — confirm with at least 2 other measures.

Bollinger %B (20,2)

Measures where price sits within a band of 2 standard deviations around a 20-day moving average. Below 0 = price is outside the lower band (oversold extreme). Above 1 = price is outside the upper band (overbought extreme).

Distance from Moving Average

% above or below the 50-day or 200-day simple moving average (SMA). The "mean" the price should revert toward. A stock 15%+ below its 50-day SMA is statistically stretched on this measure.

Z-Score vs 50-day mean

How many standard deviations the current price is from its 50-day average. Beyond ±2.0 is historically extreme — price tends to revert toward 0 over the following days/weeks.

Oversold + High IV → Premium Selling

Oversold stock + elevated implied volatility (IV) = ideal for cash-secured puts or bull put credit spreads. You sell inflated IV, and if the stock doesn't fall further, you keep the premium. Always defined risk — never naked.

Oversold + Low IV → Buying Structures

When IV is low, options are cheap — good for bull call spreads or long calls. You pay less premium to define your risk on the long side.

Overbought + High IV → Bear Call Spread

Overbought stock + elevated IV = sell an OTM call / buy a further OTM call. You profit if the stock stays below your short strike. Defines your max loss to the debit paid. Never naked short calls.

Bull Put Credit Spread

Sell a put at strike A (higher) / buy a put at strike B (lower). You collect a net credit. Max profit = credit received. Max loss = spread width minus credit. Profits if stock stays above your short strike by expiration.

Bear Call Credit Spread

Sell a call at strike A (lower) / buy a call at strike B (higher). You collect a net credit. Max profit = credit received. Max loss = spread width minus credit. Profits if stock stays below your short strike by expiration.

Bull Call Debit Spread

Buy a call at strike A / sell a call at strike B (higher). You pay a net debit. Best in low-IV environments. Max loss = debit paid. Max profit = spread width minus debit. Profits if stock rises above your long strike.

IV = Implied Volatility

The market's best guess at how much a stock might move. High IV = expensive options (better to sell). Low IV = cheap options (better to buy). IV typically rises on fear events and falls as calm returns (IV crush).

Defined Risk — Always

Every structure in this report is defined-risk: you know your maximum loss before you enter. Naked options (unlimited loss potential) are never recommended here. Paper-trade all strategies before risking real capital.

8How to Read This / Guardrails

The most important filter in the whole scan is separating a "stretched stock that will bounce" from a "falling knife that will keep falling." Here’s how we try to do that, and where the limits are.

The Reversion-vs-Ruin Filter
A low RSI is necessary but not sufficient. We reject a name and label it a potential ruin if:
  • The drop is driven by fraud, going-concern, bankruptcy, failed drug trial, or accounting restatement — these are permanent re-ratings, not mean reversions.
  • The drop is 50%+ in days with no stabilization — more likely to continue than bounce.
  • It is a buyout / merger repricing — the stock is pinned to a deal price, not its mean.
We keep names where the move looks like sentiment/positioning excess over an otherwise sound business, ideally near a prior support level. The semiconductor names today pass this test: the selloff is described by multiple analysts as "a positioning-driven unwind" at the sector level, not a deterioration in AI fundamentals.
Special Handling — Volatility, Leveraged & Futures ETFs
  • Long-vol ETPs (VXX, UVXY): Only trade them when stretched UP on a fear spike. Never buy them as an "oversold" play — structural roll decay (contango) grinds them lower permanently. Today VXX is at a 52-week low. Do not buy.
  • Leveraged ETFs (TQQQ, TNA, UCO, BOIL, etc.): Volatility decay + daily rebalancing means they deviate from any stable long-run mean. Use for very short-term tactical trades only. Multi-week holds in leveraged ETFs risk significant decay losses even if the underlying goes your way.
  • Futures-based ETFs (USO, UNG, BOIL, etc.): Subject to contango (roll cost drags performance) or backwardation (roll tailwind). USO and UNG both issue K-1 tax forms — check with your tax advisor. Prefer PDBC, BCI, or XLE as no-K-1 alternatives where available.
  • Proxy ETFs (GDX, GDXJ, URA, XOP, OIH, COPX): These track companies (miners, drillers, producers), NOT the underlying commodity. They carry equity beta — they can diverge from spot prices significantly. GDX can fall even when gold is flat if mining costs rise or management disappoints.
