7 Options Structures Legend

This legend explains the generic method — how each structure works and why. The specific setup results (which stock, which direction, which strike) live in the tables above.

RSI (0–100) Measures recent price momentum. Below 30 = recent selling was extreme (oversold — a "stretched" reading suggesting a possible bounce). Above 70 = recent buying was extreme (overbought — possible pullback). Below 20 / above 80 = more extreme. RSI alone is not enough — use it with other signals.
Bollinger %B Tells you where price sits relative to its normal range (a 20-day moving average ± 2 standard deviations). Below 0 = price below the lower band (oversold). Above 1 = price above the upper band (overbought). Pairs with RSI for confirmation.
Z-score How many standard deviations price is from its 50-day average. A Z-score of –2 or better means the price is unusually far below average — statistically stretched. Above +2 is the overbought equivalent.
% vs 50-SMA/200-SMA How far (as a percentage) today's price is from its 50-day or 200-day simple moving average — the "mean" the price might revert toward. A bigger gap = more stretched. The 50-day is the near-term mean; 200-day is the long-term anchor.
IV (Implied Volatility) The options market's expectation of future price swings. Elevated IV means options are expensive — better for selling premium (credit spreads, cash-secured puts). Low IV means options are cheap — better for buying (debit spreads, long calls/puts). Reversion + high IV = the ideal premium-selling setup.
Bull Call Spread Buy a lower-strike call, sell a higher-strike call in the same expiration. Defined risk (max loss = premium paid). For oversold setups when IV is elevated (cheaper to spread than buy outright). Profit if stock rises to the higher strike.
Bear Put Spread Buy a higher-strike put, sell a lower-strike put in the same expiration. Defined risk. For overbought setups. Profit if stock falls to the lower strike. Costs less than a naked long put.
Put Credit Spread Sell a higher-strike put, buy a lower-strike put (protection). Collect net premium. For oversold setups with elevated IV — you're paid to bet the stock stays above the sold strike. Defined risk = the spread width minus premium received.
Call Credit Spread Sell a lower-strike call, buy a higher-strike call (protection). For overbought setups with elevated IV. Defined risk = spread width minus premium received.
Cash-Secured Put Sell a put and hold cash to buy shares if assigned. For strongly oversold, fundamentally-sound stocks at support you'd be comfortable owning. Elevated IV makes the premium received larger. Maximum loss = strike price minus premium (if stock goes to zero).
Defined-Risk First All structures above have a maximum loss that is known in advance. Never use naked (uncovered) calls or puts in a reversion framework — the thesis can be wrong, and an unlimited-loss structure can be catastrophic. Always paper-trade first.

8 How to Read / Guardrails

Reversion vs. Ruin — the most important filter in this entire report.

A low RSI is necessary but not sufficient for a reversion play. This filter was applied to every name above. Here's how to apply it yourself:

  • Keep it when: the selloff looks like sentiment/positioning excess over a fundamentally-intact business. The "mean" is well-defined. The stock is near prior support. Example: ORCL, CRM, WDAY sold off on IBM guilt-by-association despite different revenue models.
  • Reject it when: the move is driven by fraud, going-concern doubt, dividend cut, failed drug trial, bankruptcy, debt blowup, or accounting restatement. Example: IBM itself is borderline — a fundamental business-model warning from the CEO is not pure sentiment.
  • IBM caution: IBM is included as a setup because RSI is extreme (~27) and the $250 support has held repeatedly. But the CEO explicitly warned of structural capex shifts — this could be a lasting re-rating, not a pure panic flush. Do NOT enter IBM before the full Q2 earnings print — verify the date.

Special rules — always apply these before trading:

  • Long-vol ETPs (VXX, UVXY): Only fade when stretched UP on a spike. Never buy the "oversold" level — structural roll decay means they drift lower permanently. Today's VXX is near 52-week lows — that is not a setup.
  • Leveraged ETFs (TBT, UCO, TQQQ, SQQQ): Volatility decay + futures roll drag mean they deviate from any long-run mean. Use for short-term tactical reversion only (days, not weeks). For multi-week holds, use the unlevered equivalent.
  • K-1 tax forms: USO, UNG, UGA, DBC, UCO, BOIL, CPER issue a Schedule K-1 — this creates tax complexity. Prefer no-K-1 alternatives where available (PDBC instead of DBC; BCI/COMB instead of broad commodity K-1 funds).
  • Equity proxies ≠ commodity spot: GDX/GDXJ track gold mining companies, not gold. XOP/OIH track oil company stocks, not crude. COPX tracks copper miners. All carry equity beta and can diverge significantly from the underlying commodity — especially during earnings season or sector rotation.
  • Earnings dates = #1 binary risk: Any setup can be obliterated by an earnings surprise. Every table above flags known near-term events. If an earnings date says "unconfirmed — verify," do exactly that before entering.
  • Paper-trade first: Options involve substantial risk. You can lose more than you invest on uncovered positions. Always confirm data in your live brokerage and simulate the trade in paper first.
