7Options Structures Legend

The table below maps the direction of the setup and the level of implied volatility (IV) to the educational defined-risk structure that typically fits best. These are educational descriptions — always confirm your brokerage's margin requirements, verify IV yourself, and paper-trade first.

📗 Oversold + High IV (e.g., PEP, NFLX, XOP)
  • Cash-Secured Put: Sell a put below support; collect premium; obligated to buy shares at the strike if assigned. Best when you'd be comfortable owning the stock at that price.
  • Bull Put Credit Spread: Sell a put at a higher strike, buy a put at a lower strike. Collect a net credit. Maximum loss = width of strikes − credit received. Defined risk. Best for RSI < 30 setups where IV is elevated (you're paid more premium).
  • Bull Call Spread: Buy a lower-strike call, sell a higher-strike call. Pay a net debit. Best when IV is moderate and you want to limit cost. Max loss = debit paid.
📕 Overbought + High IV (e.g., MU, WDFC, UNG)
  • Bear Call Credit Spread: Sell a call at a lower strike, buy a call at a higher strike. Collect a net credit. Maximum loss = width of strikes − credit received. Best for RSI > 70 setups where IV is elevated — you profit if the stock stays below the short strike.
  • Bear Put Spread: Buy a higher-strike put, sell a lower-strike put. Pay a net debit. Best when IV is moderate. Profits as price falls.
📊 How to read the technical gauges used in this report
  • RSI (Relative Strength Index, 14-day): A 0–100 momentum gauge. Below 30 = oversold; above 70 = overbought. Extreme readings: below 20 or above 80.
  • Bollinger %B: Measures where price sits within its volatility band. Below 0 = below the lower band (oversold); above 1 = above the upper band (overbought).
  • % from 50/200d SMA: How far the current price is from its 50-day or 200-day moving average (a measure of "how stretched" the price is from its baseline).
  • Z-score: How many standard deviations the price is from its 50-day mean. Readings beyond ±2 are statistically unusual.
  • Composite score (3/5–5/5): How many of the five measures (RSI, Bollinger %B, % from 50d, % from 200d, consecutive-day streak/ATR) agree that the name is stretched. 5/5 = most confident; 3/5 = single-indicator — lower conviction.
⚠ IV (Implied Volatility) — why it matters for structure choice
  • High IV means options are expensive — option sellers collect more premium. Favors credit spreads and cash-secured puts.
  • Low IV means options are cheap — option buyers pay less. Favors debit spreads (buying spreads) if you expect a directional move.
  • IV crush risk: After earnings or big events, IV typically collapses — if you bought options before the event, you can lose even if you were directionally right. Selling premium before known events carries its own risk if the move is larger than expected.

8How to Read This Report / Guardrails

⚠ THE REVERSION-VS-RUIN FILTER — the most important rule in this framework
A low RSI is necessary but not sufficient for a reversion trade. A name can stay oversold forever if the business is broken. This report rejects any name where the decline is driven by:
  • Structural breaks: Fraud, going-concern doubt, failed drug trial, bankruptcy, accounting restatement
  • Catastrophic decline: −50%+ in a few days with no stabilization (likely more to come)
  • Buyout repricing: Stock pinned to a deal price, not reverting to a mean
Keep: Names where the move looks like sentiment/positioning excess over an otherwise sound business, ideally near a prior support level.
Special handling rules for non-standard ETFs:
  • Long-Vol ETPs (VXX, UVXY): Trade only when stretched UP (spike-fade). Never buy them as "oversold" — structural contango roll decay grinds them lower indefinitely.
  • Inverse-Vol ETPs (SVXY, SVIX): These grind up over time and crater on vol spikes. Do not fade their uptrend; their reversion setup is recovery after a spike.
  • Leveraged ETFs (TQQQ, TBT, UCO, BOIL, etc.): Volatility decay + daily rebalancing = NO stable long-run mean. Use only for short-term tactical trades (1–3 days). Never hold multi-week.
  • K-1 Tax Flag (USO, UNG, UCO, BOIL, DBC, etc.): These issue a Schedule K-1, not a 1099. This adds tax complexity. Prefer no-K-1 alternatives: PDBC (broad commodity), BCI, XLE, XOP.
  • Equity Proxies (GDX, XOP, COPX, URA, etc.): These track companies, not the commodity. They carry equity beta and can diverge significantly from the underlying commodity. Always noted as "proxy" in the tables.
