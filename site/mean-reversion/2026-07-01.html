7Options Structures Legend

This legend explains the method — what the indicators mean and why certain structures fit certain setups. The specific results (RSI readings, structures recommended) are in the tables above.

Indicators — what they measure

RSI (0–100 overbought/oversold gauge)

14-day Relative Strength Index. Below 30 = potentially oversold (selling may be exhausted). Above 70 = potentially overbought (buying may be exhausted). Extreme reads: <20 or >80. RSI alone is not enough — use with other signals.

Bollinger %B (band position gauge)

Where price sits relative to its 20-day Bollinger Band. Below 0 = price is outside the lower band (very stretched down). Above 1 = price is outside the upper band (very stretched up). Band breaks often precede reversals.

% from 50/200-day SMA (distance gauge)

How far price is above or below its 50- or 200-day moving average (the reference "mean"). The further from the mean, the more stretched. A return toward the SMA is the reversion target.

Z-Score (standard deviation distance)

How many standard deviations price is from its 50-day average. Z-score >2 = extreme overbought; <−2 = extreme oversold. Used to rank composite stretch — 3+ signals is stronger than RSI alone.

The scan's filters and special rules — applied before any name makes it onto a table above.

🚫 Reversion vs. Ruin filter (most important)

A low RSI is necessary but not sufficient. We reject: names with structural breaks (fraud, going-concern, failed trials, bankruptcy, accounting restatement), drops of −50%+ in days with no stabilization (likely more to come), or buyout/merger repricing (stock is pinned to a deal price, not a mean). Every name above cleared this filter — moves appear to be sentiment/positioning excess, not permanent re-ratings. When in doubt, the conservative call is to wait for stabilization.

⚠ Falling knives get cut

If a name appears to be in a structural downtrend with no clear support and no identifiable "normal" to revert to, it is dropped. The mean-reversion framework requires a well-defined reference point. Event-driven extremes (like the Iran war oil spike) provide the clearest reference point — the pre-shock price.

📄 K-1 Tax Flag

USO, UNG, UGA, BNO, DBC, UCO, SCO, BOIL, KOLD, and most CurrencyShares ETFs issue a Schedule K-1 tax form instead of a 1099. This adds complexity at tax time. Where the exposure allows, prefer no-K-1 alternatives: PDBC, BCI, COMB (broad commodity), SCO (2x inverse oil — no K-1 for this one — verify with your broker).

📈 Proxy Flag

GDX, GDXJ, XOP, COPX, LIT, URA, URNM, MOO, SIL track companies, not the commodity directly. They carry equity beta and can diverge significantly from spot prices. A gold miner can fall even when gold rises (cost inflation, management issues). Label says "PROXY" — factor in equity-specific risk.

⚡ Leveraged & Futures ETFs — short-term only

SCO, UCO, BOIL, KOLD, TQQQ, SQQQ, TBT, TMF, and all 2x/3x funds suffer from volatility decay and futures roll costs (contango/backwardation). They deviate from long-run means. Use for short-term tactical reversion only (days to a few weeks). For multi-week holds, use the unlevered fund or equity-sector proxy.

🔒 Long-vol ETPs — spike-fade only, never "oversold" buys

VXX, UVXY, VIXY are long-volatility instruments that structurally decay from VIX futures contango. Their RSI being low is drift, not a setup. The only trade is fading them when they spike up. Today, VIX is calm (~17.65) — no spike exists to fade. Never buy VXX/UVXY because they look "cheap." See Section 6.
